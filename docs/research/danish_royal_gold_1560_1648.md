# Danish royal gold coinage 1560–1648 — phases and standards

> **Research note** — broader survey of Danish royal gold issuance from
> Frederik II's 1563 Syvårskrigen Klippen onwards to Christian IV's
> death in 1648. Compiled 2026-05-14 as the umbrella context for §AB
> (Daler-Klippe 1604-05) and a follow-up to that dossier's §4 pattern
> discussion. Resolves how multiple coexisting gold standards (each
> with its own fineness regime and tariff) phase across this 88-year
> window, and which fit established Fuß categories vs which form
> own standards.
>
> **Companion dossier**: `docs/research/daler_klippe_1604.md` —
> deep dive on the 1604-05 Daler-Klippe specifically. This document
> places that case in its broader Danish royal-gold context.

---

## TL;DR

Five distinct gold-fineness regimes coexisted in Danish royal coinage
between 1560 and 1648. They are NOT a single «tradition» — they're
parallel tracks, some politically motivated (war finance, regency
defense, royal prestige), some imperial-imitative (Reichsdukatenfuß,
Rhenish Gulden), some Klippen-format presentation pieces.

| Fineness | Standard | Period | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0.986** | **Reichsdukatenfuß** | 1563–1648 | Frederik II f2h1/f2h4 (Ungersk Gylden, Dukat); Christian IV Haderslev c4h8 (Ungersk Gylden) |
| **0.979** | Reichsdukatenfuß-derived (slight debasement) | 1605–1648 | Christian IV c4h9 (Portugaloser 1605); c4h30 (1/4 Portugaloser 1629); c4h32-c4h40 (Dukat series 1644-1648) |
| **0.972** | Reichsdukatenfuß-debased (~1.4% deficit) | 1603–1611 | Christian IV c4h14, c4h15, c4h17, c4h18 (Ungersk Gylden + 2 Ungersk Gylden, Copenhagen) |
| **0.934** | **Frederik-II-Guldkrone-fod** | 1563–1564 only | f2h2, f2h5 (1 Guldkrone) |
| **0.917** | **Christian-IV-Guldkrone-fod (Corona Danica)** | 1619–1648 | c4h25, c4h26, c4h28 (Guldkrone, 2 Guldkrone) |
| **0.833** | **par-metal Klippen sub-cluster** | 1604–1629 | c4h12 (4 Daler 1604); c4h23, c4h24 (Rosenobel 1611-1629) |
| **0.937 / 0.923** | **Daler-Klippe 1604 own standard** | 1604–1605 only | c4h10 (8 Daler), c4h11 (6 Daler) — see §AB dossier |
| **0.906** | Heavy-Mark gold piece (one-off) | 1560/1563 | f2h8 (3 Mark gold, 26.49g fein) |
| **0.77** | **Rhinsk Gylden (German tradition)** | 1563–1632 | f2h3, f2h6 (Frederik II); c4h29 (Christian IV 1625-1632) |

**Key insights:**
- The **Klippen format itself** is Frederik-II-1563-vintage, not Christian-IV-1604. Frederik II's Bremerholm workshop struck «curious gold clippings» (per danskmoent.dk/f2flens.htm) during Syvårskrigen — gold Klippen for war finance / Soldzahlung.
- **Three distinct «Guldkrone» standards** existed: Frederik II 0.934 (1563-64), Frederik III later 0.917 (1657-1668 main phase per project's existing guldkrone Fuß), Christian IV 0.917 (1619-1648, predecessor phase to Frederik III's standard). Frederik II's 1563-64 is a **standalone non-continuous standard**.
- The **0.833 «par-metal Klippen sub-cluster»** spans 1604-1629 (Daler-Klippe 1604 4-Daler + Rosenobel 1611-1629 series) — this is a real recurring standard, not coincidence. Likely the «par-metal ordinance-defined gold» tradition lives at this fineness.
- **Reichsdukatenfuß proper (0.986)** survived intact for Ungersk Gylden + Dukat throughout, with gradual debasement to 0.979 by 1605 and stabilising there for late Dukat.
- The «1590 Prinsens Daler» Wilcke II mention is most likely **silver** (Hede c4h43 — «1 Speciedaler 1590», fein 27.4g at 0.937 fineness, a proto-Speciedaler from the regency before formal coronation 1596).

## 1. Frederik II era 1559-1588

### 1.1 Bremerholm goldsmith workshop and three gold types

Per **danskmoent.dk/f2flens.htm** (Frederik II's coinage history):

> «three different gold types» were minted at **Bremerholm** (the
> king's goldsmith workshop on Holmen), specifically «Hungarian,
> Rhenish, and Crown gold», with mintmasters **Hans Willers,
> Hans Köpelin, and Laudris Hammer** producing «curious gold
> clippings» where value was marked as «I GOLT GILDEN, I DOK KATE
> or I GOLT KRONE».

This passage is foundational. It establishes that:

1. **The Klippen gold format originates with Frederik II's Bremerholm operation**, not with Christian IV 1604.
2. **Three parallel gold standards** coexisted simultaneously in
   1563-1564 (Ungersk Gylden / Rhinsk Gylden / Guldkrone), each at
   its own fineness.
3. **Three mintmasters** ran the gold workshop in parallel. (Hans Willers is cited on Hede f2h2 as mintmaster of the 4 860-stk Guldkrone series.)
4. The **Reverse legends** were explicitly nominal-marked: «I GOLT GILDEN» (1 Hungarian Gulden), «I DOK KATE» (1 Dukat — Reichsdukat variant), «I GOLT KRONE» (1 Guldkrone). Clear unit-of-account branding.

### 1.2 Syvårskrigen context — war finance through coinage

From **danskmoent.dk's monetary history pages** (search results 2026-05-14):

> «In 1563, Frederik demanded that the Swedes acknowledge his use
> of the Swedish three crowns in his coat of arms, which was a
> clear provocation and declaration of war. Frederik II declared
> war on Sweden in 1563.»

> «During the Northern Seven Years' War (1563-70), Frederik II had
> clipper coins minted with denominations of 2 and 4 skilling as
> well as 1 and 2 mark. These clippings were deficient and/or
> underweight, meaning they contained too little silver in
> relation to their nominal value.»

> «The production and distribution of such deficient and/or
> underweight coins provided quick profit to the king's treasury,
> and the profits went especially to war financing in 1563, not
> least for payment of mercenary troops.»

The silver Klippen for war finance are well-documented as **Tarifmünze
in the Def-1 sense** (face value > metal value, state seigniorage).
The **gold Klippen of the same period (1563-64 Guldkrone, Ungersk
Gylden, Rhinsk Gylden) likely served a parallel function** —
royal-prestige gold for diplomatic gifts, mercenary commanders'
payments at face-value-above-melt rates.

### 1.4 Standards analysis

**0.986 Reichsdukatenfuß (Ungersk Gylden + Dukat 1563-64)**

These ARE at the imperial Reichsdukatenfuß standard. 1 Ungersk Gylden
= 1 Reichsdukat by composition (3.442 g fein). Tariff value within
Denmark at the time: 1 Dukat ≈ 1 Reichsthaler = silver equivalent at
gold-silver ratio. Likely par-metal commercial gold for international
trade settlement.

**0.934 «Frederik-II-Guldkrone-fod» (Guldkrone 1563-64 only)**

Distinct standard. Per-coin fein: 3.120 g. NOT 3.44 g (Reichsdukat).
NOT 3.67 g (theoretical Cölln. Mark 233.856 / 64 if 64 Guldkrone per
Mark). The 3.120 g fein figure is the **Krone-fod** standard
(unverified what Mark-fein divisor produces it):

  - 233.856 g fein per Cölln. Marck ÷ 3.120 g per Guldkrone = **74.95
    Guldkrone per Marck fein**. Not an obvious round number — could be
    «75 Guldkrone per Marck fein» convention with slight specimen
    variance.

The 0.934 fineness ≈ 22½-Karat (22.5/24 = 0.9375 — within specimen
variance). 22-Karat gold ≈ 0.9166, 23-Karat ≈ 0.9583. Likely a
deliberate 22½-Karat alloy chosen for the «Crown» series.

**Connection to Christian IV's later Guldkrone (1619+)**: different
fineness (0.917 vs 0.934), different fein per piece (2.725 g vs
3.120 g). NOT a continuous standard. Christian IV's 1618 Corona
Danica patent reset the Guldkrone parameters.

**0.77 Rhinsk Gylden (Rhinsk standard)**

Rhinsk Gulden = Rhenish Gulden = post-1559 imperial-tier gold at
deliberately-lower fineness vs. Reichsdukat. 0.77 ≈ 18.5-karat. Used
in German-imperial trade alongside the 0.986 Dukat. Mintage was
likely small (specific German-trade-oriented series).

**0.906 / 26.49 g fein 3 Mark (one-off heavy gold)**

f2h8 is unique — «3 Mark» in gold at 0.906 fineness, 26.49 g fein,
struck 1560 and 1563. Not a denomination-set member; isolated piece.
The 26.49 g fein is close to one Cölln. Marck fein × 1/9 (= 25.98 g)
or thereabouts — possibly an experimental piece testing a heavy gold
denomination tied to silver Daler value via gold-silver ratio.

## 2. Christian IV regency 1588-1596 — Haderslev gold under Sophie / Metzner

### 2.1 Political context

Christian IV ascended at age 11 in 1588; mature rule from 1596
coronation. During the regency the state was governed by Queen
Dowager Sophie of Mecklenburg (his mother) and a council of
nobles. Per **Harck 2011** (cited on Hede c4h1):

> «Et forsvarsskrift for enkedronning Sofie» — defense memorandum
> for Queen Dowager Sophie.

The Haderslev gold series 1591-1593 was a **political instrument**
asserting Sophie's monetary regalia / dynastic position. Not a
standard «war finance» Klippen; not pure presentation. **Political
defense piece** with gold-coin-issuance prerogative.

### 2.2 Per-coin inventory (Hede c4h1-c4h8)

Mintmaster: **Andreas Metzner** (per WebFetch c4h1, mark crescent
moon). Mint: **Haderslev** (Hadersleben, Schleswig-Holstein duchy
— NOT royal Copenhagen mint).

|---|---|---|---|
| c4h1 | 1 + 1/2 Portugaloser | 1591–1592 | Rarity RR / Unik |
| c4h2 | (not cached, presumably missing or sub-letter) | — | — |
| c4h3 | 1/4 Portugaloser | 1592 | — |
| c4h4 | 2 Portugaloser | 1592 | Brutto 69.74 g (≈2× normal Portugaloser) |

| c4h8 | 1 Ungersk Gylden | 1591–1593 (Unik / RRR / RR) | Finhed 0.986 — Reichsdukatenfuß proper |

### 2.3 Standards analysis

**Reichsdukatenfuß proper (c4h8 Ungersk Gylden)**

Same as Frederik II 1563-64 Ungersk Gylden: fineness 0.986, fein
3.442 g per piece. **Continuous imperial Reichsdukatenfuß standard
inherited from Frederik II**.

**Portugaloser (c4h1-c4h7)**

Hede cache lacks finhed for Portugaloser, but the brutto-weight
relationship gives a clue:

  - 1 Portugaloser brutto = 34.88 g (c4h5)
  - Per-coin fein at Reichsdukatenfuß 0.986 = 34.88 × 0.986 = 34.39 g
  - 10 × 3.44 (10-Dukat tariff) = 34.4 g → matches!
  - **Portugaloser = 10-Dukat denomination at Reichsdukatenfuß**

The 2 Portugaloser (c4h4 brutto 69.74 g) follows same logic: 20-Dukat
piece. The 1/2 and 1/4 fractions are correspondingly 5-Dukat and
2.5-Dukat pieces.

Conclusion: **all Haderslev 1591-1593 gold fits Reichsdukatenfuß**
proper, just in unusual large-multiple denominations + Ungersk
Gylden 1-Dukat. **Not a separate Fuß.** Politically distinct
issuance, but metallurgically standard.

## 3. Christian IV mature reign 1596-1648

### 3.1 Phasing

After 1596 coronation Christian IV operates the royal Copenhagen
mint with successive mintmasters. Key phases:

- **Pre-Schwabe 1596-1602**: silver Speciedaler / 1 Mark / Skilling
  output at Copenhagen. Gold output minimal. Hede c4h43 (1 Speciedaler
  1590) is the «1590 Prinsens Daler» Wilcke II referenced — actually a
  silver coin, mint not specified (likely transitional / pre-coronation
  piece). Brutto 29.232 g at 0.937 fineness → fein 27.405 g — higher
  fineness than later 9-Fuß Reichsthaler (0.889).
- **Schwabe 1602-1608**: 1602 Møntordning, first ordinance gold (1604
  Daler-Klippe, see §AB dossier). Copenhagen Ungersk Gylden continues
  at slightly debased 0.972.
- **Kalmar War 1611-1613**: Rosenobel series introduced for war
  finance (gold imitation of English Noble).
- **Inter-war 1613-1618**: silver re-stabilisation, Kronemønt patent
  preparation.
- **Corona Danica 1618-1648**: silver Krone tariff system + parallel
  Guldkrone series.
- **Late mature 1644-1648**: formal Reichsdukatenfuß proper Dukat
  series (post-Speciedaler-formalisation 1644).

### 3.2 Reichsdukatenfuß track 1603–1648

The continuous «imperial gold» track. Per-issue specs from Hede
cache:

| c4h15 | 1 Ungersk Gylden | 1604 | København | 0.972 | 3.393 g |

| c4h38, c4h40 | 1/2 Dukat | 1647 | København | 0.979 | 1.709 g |

**Fineness drift**: 0.986 (Frederik II + Haderslev 1591-93) → 0.972
(Copenhagen 1603-1611, slight debasement, ~1.4 % deficit) → **0.979
(1605 + 1629 + 1644-1648)** stabilises here.

**Per-Dukat fein**: 3.418 g (late Dukat) ≈ 3.44 g canonical. The fein
stays constant; only fineness drifts. Brutto adjusts to compensate.

**Conclusion**: 0.979 is essentially **Reichsdukatenfuß proper with
small fixed Danish-mint adjustment** — call it «Reichsdukatenfuß
(Danish-mint variant)» or just accept ~1 % debasement vs. imperial
standard. Either way, this track fits our existing
`reichsdukatenfuss` Fuß category as long as we allow ~1-2 % fineness
tolerance.

### 3.3 Daler-Klippe 1604-05 own standard

Covered in detail in **`docs/research/daler_klippe_1604.md`**. Brief
recap:

- Hede c4h10 (8 Daler), c4h11 (6 Daler), c4h12 (4 Daler), c4h13 (3 Daler)
- Variable fineness 0.937 / 0.923 / 0.833 / unknown
- Per-Daler fein constant ≈ 2.03 g
- Tariff: 1 Daler = 0.583 Dukat (6 Daler = 3.5 Hungarian Gulden)
- Par-metal at tariff; NOT Def-1 Tarifmünze
- Forordning 25 June + 8 September 1602
- **Own standard** — not Reichsdukatenfuß

### 3.4 Rosenobel 1611-1629 — Kalmar-War gold

Hede c4h23 (1 Rosenobel), c4h24 (1/2 Rosenobel).

| Hede | Nominal | Years | Brutto | Finhed | Fein |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| c4h23 | 1 Rosenobel | 1611, 1612, 1613, 1627, 1629 | 8.994 g | 0.833 | 7.495 g |
| c4h24 | 1/2 Rosenobel | 1611 | 4.497 g | 0.833 | 3.748 g |

**Fineness 0.833** — same as 4 Daler 1604 (c4h12). Coincidence or
deliberate standard? Brutto / fein cleanly halves between 1 and 1/2
Rosenobel — consistent within the series.

Historical context: **Kalmar War 1611-1613** between Denmark and
Sweden. Rosenobel = «Rose Noble», continental imitation of English
Noble gold coin (originally 6s 8d face value in England). Christian
IV's Rosenobel was issued for war finance (Soldzahlung).

**Tariff value**: unclear from Hede cache. Likely 2-3 Speciedaler
based on per-coin fein content (~2.18 Reichsdukaten = ~2.04 silver
Daler at 12:1 ratio, so ~2 silver Daler par-metal). Needs external
research to confirm tariff.

**Standard**: 0.833 fineness suggests deliberate sub-Dukat
standard. NOT Reichsdukatenfuß. The fein 7.495 g per Rosenobel
divides Cölln. Marck fein into 233.856 / 7.495 = **31.2 Rosenobel
per Marck fein** — not an obvious Fuß figure. Possibly «31 1/5» or
«31 Rosenobel per Mark fein» convention.

**Continuity claim — par-metal Klippen sub-cluster**: the 0.833
fineness recurs across 4 Daler 1604 (c4h12) AND Rosenobel 1611-1629
AND c4h23b 1629 Rosenobel — this is a **persistent gold standard**
that appears whenever Christian IV's mint produces ordinance-defined
non-Reichsdukatenfuß Klippen. May warrant its own Fuß category
distinct from the 1604 Daler-Klippe and from Guldkrone.

### 3.5 Christian IV's Guldkrone (Corona Danica) 1619-1648

Hede c4h25, c4h26, c4h28:

| Hede | Nominal | Years | Brutto | Finhed | Fein |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| c4h25 | 2 Guldkrone | 1619, 1621, 1626, 1628, 1630, 1633, 1635, 1637, 1648 | 5.996 g | 0.917 | 5.496 g |
| c4h26 | 1 Guldkrone | 1619 | 2.973 g | 0.917 | 2.725 g |
| c4h28 | 2 Guldkrone | 1628-1629 | 5.996 g | 0.917 | 5.496 g |

**Fineness 0.917** (22-karat). Per-Guldkrone fein: 2.725 g. NOT same
as Frederik II 1563-64 Guldkrone (which was 0.934 / fein 3.120 g) —
the standards differ.

Per project's existing `guldkrone` Fuß: Frederik III's 1655-1668
main phase has tariff «1 Guldkrone = 3.5 Speciedaler». Christian
IV's 1619-1648 predecessor phase uses **the same metric** (0.917
fineness, 5.996g brutto / 5.496g fein per 2-Guldkrone, half for
single Guldkrone) — already established in our `data/locations/
denmark.yml` guldkrone phase, dossier confirms.

**Conclusion**: Christian IV Guldkrone 1619-1648 IS within our
existing `guldkrone` Fuß category. Specifically the «Christian IV
predecessor phase» our existing data describes.

### 3.6 Christian IV Rhinsk Gylden 1625-1632

Hede c4h29: Christian IV 1 Rhinsk Gylden 1625, 1627, 1628, 1632.
Brutto 3.249 g, finhed 0.76, fein 2.47 g. **Same standard as
Frederik II 1563-64 Rhinsk Gylden** (0.77, fein 2.5 g — slight
specimen variation). German Rhenish Gulden continues through
Christian IV's reign for German trade context.

**Standard**: 0.76-0.77 fineness Rhenish Gulden. Distinct from
Reichsdukatenfuß; distinct from Guldkrone-fod; distinct from
Daler-Klippe / Rosenobel. **Own standard** — call it
«rhinsk_gylden_fod» or «Rhenish-Gulden-Fuß».

### 5.1 Open Fuß-design questions

1. **Frederik II Guldkrone 1563-64 vs Christian IV Guldkrone 1619+**:
   different fineness (0.934 vs 0.917), different per-coin fein
   (3.120 vs 2.725 g), 55-year gap with NO Guldkrone strikes between.
   These are NOT a continuous standard. Two options:
   - (a) Create separate Fuß `f2_guldkrone_fod` (1563-64 only) +
     keep existing `guldkrone` for 1619+ (Christian IV / Frederik III).
   - (b) Add a phase to the existing `guldkrone` Fuß for the 1563-64
     case, named «Frederik II Guldkrone-fod» as a phase rather than
     a separate Fuß — but the metric break (0.934 → 0.917) makes
     phase modelling awkward.

2. **Rhinsk Gylden** is its own Fuß family — appears in both Frederik
   II (1563-64) and Christian IV (1625-1632) data. **New Fuß
   `rhinsk_gylden_fod` recommended**. Tariff: traditionally 1 Rhinsk
   Gylden ≈ 0.7-0.75 Reichsdukat (worth less in fein than Dukat).

3. **Rosenobel + 0.833 par-metal Klippen sub-cluster**: spans
   1604-1629 (4 Daler 1604 + Rosenobel 1611-1629). Same fineness,
   different tariff. Could be:
   - (a) Single Fuß `par_metal_klippen` covering both — would conflate
     two different tariffs.
   - (b) Two separate Fuß categories — `daler_klippen_1604` (4 Daler
     fits here too, alongside 8/6/3 Daler from §AB) and
     `rosenobel_fod` (1611-1629).
   - (c) Generic «non-Fuß ordinance gold» category at schema level.

4. **0.972 vs 0.979 vs 0.986 Reichsdukatenfuß tolerance**: technically
   these are different fineness levels. Our project allows ~2 %
   tolerance per §4 verification rule. Treating all three as the
   same `reichsdukatenfuss` Fuß is justified within tolerance; the
   debasement signal is research-relevant but doesn't break the
   Fuß abstraction.

## 6. The «1590 Prinsens Daler» mystery — resolved

Wilcke II references mention «1590 Prinsens Daler» as preceding the
Haderslev gold series. Our Hede cache has **Hede c4h43 «1 Speciedaler
1590»** — a single silver entry at brutto 29.232 g, finhed 0.937,
fein 27.405 g. This is **silver, not gold**.

The 27.405 g fein figure is HIGHER than:
- 9-Fuß Reichsthaler (25.984 g fein at 0.889)
- Frederik II's earlier silver Mark issues

It's the **silver-Daler standard before the 1602 reform**, before
the formal 1644 Speciedaler standard, before the 9-Fuß became
formalised for Denmark. A **proto-Speciedaler** at 0.937 fineness.

**Conclusion**: Wilcke II's «1590 Prinsens Daler» is **silver
c4h43** — not gold. It's a pre-coronation princely silver Daler at
a transitional standard. Should be classified under early 9-Fuß or
own pre-9-Fuß silver category. Not part of the gold-tradition this
dossier covers. The mystery is resolved as **wrong-metal confusion
in my earlier §AB dossier §4**.

## 7. Phase summary — the three-phase model

Combining all evidence, Danish royal gold 1560-1648 splits into
three broad phases:

### Phase I: Frederik II Bremerholm tri-standard (1559-1588)

Three coexisting gold types at the Copenhagen Bremerholm workshop
(Ungersk Gylden, Guldkrone, Rhinsk Gylden) plus one-off heavy gold
(3 Mark). Mintmasters Hans Willers, Hans Köpelin, Laudris Hammer.
Klippen format documented for the «curious gold clippings» (per
f2flens). Syvårskrigen 1563-70 drives mintage.

### Phase II: Christian IV regency Haderslev (1588-1596)

Single mint (Haderslev, Schleswig-Holstein duchy), single mintmaster
(Andreas Metzner), single Fuß (Reichsdukatenfuß proper at 0.986),
multiple denominations (Portugaloser fractions + Ungersk Gylden).
Political instrument — defense memorandum for Queen Dowager Sophie
(Harck 2011).

### Phase III: Christian IV mature reign Copenhagen (1596-1648)

Multi-track gold issuance at royal Copenhagen mint under successive
mintmasters (Schwabe from 1602, later successors). Five parallel
tracks coexisting:
- Reichsdukatenfuß proper (Ungersk Gylden + Dukat + Portugaloser
  Copenhagen, 1603-1648)
- Daler-Klippe ordinance-gold (1604-1605, own standard)
- Rosenobel war finance (1611-1629, own standard)
- Guldkrone Corona Danica (1619-1648, own standard)
- Rhinsk Gylden German-trade (1625-1632, continuation of F2 standard)

This is the **richest phase by far** — five gold standards in 50 years.
