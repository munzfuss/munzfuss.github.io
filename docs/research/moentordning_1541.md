# Møntordningen af 20. september 1541 (Christian III)

> **Type:** analytical research dossier.
>
> **Anchor for:** Danish numismatic project — the 1541 ordinance is the

## 1. Identification

- **Date issued**: 20 September 1541.
- **Issuing authority**: Christian III, King of Denmark and Norway (r. 1534–1559).
- **Place of issue**: København.
- **Type**: Royal ordinance (Møntordning / monetary edict).
- **Companion act**: a supplementary ordinance dated 1544 (Galster: «1544»;
  one secondary source gives 27 September 1544) that places the daler at
  the head of the monetary system. The 1541 + 1544 pair is consistently
  treated by Galster, Wilcke and Kroman as a single legislative arc.
- **Distinct from**: the Reformation ordinance («Recessen om reformationen»,
  30 October 1536) — that's an ecclesiastical instrument, separate from
  the monetary track.

## 3. Verbatim Galster transcription (the canonical paraphrase)

From `docs/research/sources/galster_galshist.md` lines 73–82 (capture of
<https://www.danskmoent.dk/galster/galshist.htm>; original: Georg
Galster, *Mønt*, København 1965, pp. 23–43):

> «Ved møntordningen af 20. sept. 1541 reorganiseredes møntvæsenet.
> Dermed indledes den nyere tid i dansk mønthistorie. Hele
> udmøntningen blev henlagt til København, hvor møntsmedjen blev
> indrettet i det nedlagte Sanct Klarekloster med Povl Fechtel som
> møntmester. For første gang sattes møntens navn i præget: mark, 8,
> 4 og 1 skilling, hvid og penning. 1544 blev daleren stillet i
> spidsen for det danske møntsystem, således at der herefter gik 3
> mark på en daler. Denne sloges 8 stk. på den 14 1/2 lødige kølnske
> mark.»

Galster also frames the broader significance in his survey opening (§1
of `christian_iii_danish_coinage_1534_1572.md` quotes this verbatim):

> «1541 — Christian III's coin ordonnance of 20 September 1541 rightly
> begins the modern period in Denmark. Christian III brought order to
> the monetary system, and the daler was now placed at the head of the
> coin system. Although he had already minted Joachimstaler in 1537,
> it was through the coin ordonnances of 1541 and 1544 that the new
> coinage system was implemented. This achieved unity both with the
> duchies, where efforts had continuously been made to maintain parity
> with the Lübeck coin, and with all of Germany, where the daler had
> completely triumphed.»

## 4. Synthesised content of the ordinance

Based on the Galster paraphrase plus cross-checks with Wilcke, Kroman
and the Hede coin record, the 1541 + 1544 ordinances together establish:

### 4.1 Institutional / administrative provisions (1541)

- **Centralised minting in København.** All Danish royal mint output
  consolidated to a single location. Earlier Christian III emergency
  output (Gottorp, Århus, Roskilde, Malmø — Grevens fejde 1534–1536)
  ends; provincial mints close. (Schleswig-Holstein duchy minting
  continues independently from this monarchy reform — the 1544
  partition of the duchies between Christian III and his brothers
  hands Gottorp + its mint to Hertug Adolph, so a new royal mint is
  established at Flensborg in 1545.)
- **Mint premises:** the dissolved Sanct Klarekloster (Saint Clare
  Convent) in København, repurposed for the monetary smithy.
  Reformation-era property reuse.
- **Mintmaster:** **Povl Fechtel** (Poul Fechtel) — head of the
  København mint from 1541, in office through 1565 (the period split
  visible in the Hede c3h record as the 3A/3B sub-letters; see §5
  below). Galster mentions Fechtel was succeeded by his son-in-law
  Hans Delhusen during the Seven Years' War (1563–1570) plague
  pressure.
- **Numismatic innovation — coin name on design.** «For første gang
  sattes møntens navn i præget» — for the FIRST time, the denomination
  name appears explicitly in the coin's design as part of the
  inscription. Prior Danish coinage relied entirely on weight, size,
  fineness and ruler iconography for identification; from 1541
  onwards the words «mark», «8 (skilling)», «4 (skilling)», «1
  skilling», «hvid», «penning» appear as legends. This is a
  significant numismatic milestone for Denmark and reflects the
  broader German practice of the period.

### 4.2 Denomination structure (1541)

The 1541 ordinance establishes the following silver-coin denomination
hierarchy (per Galster's enumeration and confirmed by the Hede c3h3-c3h8
catalogue):

| Denomination | Galster label | Hede page (København) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 Mark | mark | c3h3 (sub-letters 3A 1541, 3B 1544–1555) |
| 8 Skilling | 8 (skilling) | c3h4 (1541–1544) |
| 4 Skilling | 4 (skilling) | c3h5 (1541–1554) |
| 1 Skilling | 1 skilling | c3h6 (1542); c3h11 (1554) |
| 1 Hvid | hvid | c3h7 (u.år 1541–1544); c3h13 (1554) |
| 1 Penning | penning | c3h8 (1546) |

Sub-coin relationships (pre-1544 baseline): 1 Mark Danske = 16 Skilling
= 192 Penning. The hvid sits between skilling and penning. After 1544
the daler is added as the top tier: 1 Daler = 3 Mark = 48 Skilling.

The «8 Skilling» denomination is notable — it is half-mark, but
catalogued by Galster (and minted) as an independent denomination
with its own legend rather than as a fractional mark. The 4 Skilling
likewise.

### 4.3 1544 supplement — daler at the head, Marken-fin 8.827

The 1544 ordinance amends 1541 by placing the **daler** at the apex of
the system:

- **1 Daler = 3 Mark Danske = 48 Skilling.**
- **Minting standard**: «8 stk. på den 14 ½ lødige kølnske mark» —
  i.e. eight Daler from one Cölln. Mark (233.856 g) of 14½-lødig
  silver. The Lødighedsgrad 14½ translates to fineness 14½ / 16 =
  0.90625, conventionally rounded to **0.906** on Hede pages.
- **Marken-fin (mf)** ≡ daler-per-Cölln.-Mark-fein:
  mf = 8 / (14½ / 16) = 8 × 16 / 14½ = 128 / 14½ ≈ **8.828**.
  Hede explicitly publishes **mf 8.827** on c3h3-3A and c3h4 / c3h5 —
  the tiny rounding gap between 8.828 and 8.827 is within the
  expected period-arithmetic precision.
- **Per-Daler silver content**: 233.856 g / 8.827 ≈ **26.494 g fein**.
- **Per-Mark silver content**: 26.494 / 3 ≈ **8.83 g fein**.

This places Christian III's 1544-codified Daler **~2 % HEAVIER** than
what would later become the imperial 9-Fuß Reichsthaler standard
(25.984 g fein per Daler, mf 9.000, Augsburger Reichsmünzordnung 1559 +
Reichsabschied 1566).

### 4.4 Internal consistency check from Hede cache

Direct attestation across the Hede c3h pages confirms the 1541 standard
applied to every denomination tier:

| Hede | Nominal | Year | Brutto (g) | Finhed | Finvægt (g) | mf | g fein / Daler equivalent |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| c3h3 sub-3A | 1 Mark | 1541 | 9.744 | **0.906** | 8.830 | **8.827** | **26.494** |
| c3h4 | 8 Skilling | 1541–1544 | 4.872 | 0.906 | 4.415 | matches | 26.494 |
| c3h5 | 4 Skilling | 1541–1554 | 2.436 | 0.906 | 2.208 | matches | 26.494 |
| c3h7 | Hvid u.år | 1541–1544 | 0.735 | 0.250 | (≈0.184) | **8.833** | 26.484 |

Hvid (smallest billon) tracks the same mf within rounding — confirming
the ordinance was a *system-wide* standard, not just for the headline
mark.

### 4.5 1544 debasement signal (Hede 3B sub-letter)

Hede catalogues a **distinct metric regime starting 1544** under the
same nominal label, marked as Hede 3B:

| Hede | Nominal | Year | Brutto | Finhed | Finvægt | mf | g fein / Daler |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| c3h3 sub-3B | 1 Mark | 1544–1555 | 9.744 | **0.843** | 8.221 | **9.481** | **24.665** |

The 1544 supplement that introduced the daler-at-head simultaneously
debased the Mark Danske by ~7 % vs the 1541 baseline (finhed
0.906 → 0.843; brutto unchanged at 9.744 g). The «daler-at-head»
provision documented by Galster therefore did NOT preserve the original
mark's silver content — the daler was a new top-tier denomination
designed at a slightly lighter standard than the implicit Daler-
equivalent of three pre-1544 Marks.

This 1541 → 1544 sub-letter split is what allows the dossier
`christian_iii_danish_coinage_1534_1572.md` §8 to recognise two
distinct phases (A1 1541–1543 baseline vs A2 1544+ debased) within
the same nominal ordinance arc.

## 5. Mints under the ordinance

- **København (Bremerholm / Sanct Klarekloster)** — the primary royal
  mint after 1541. Operates 1541 onwards under Povl Fechtel; full
  Christian III silver catalogue (c3h3-c3h13) is København-issued.
- **Flensborg** — re-established 1545 (after the 1544 partition of
  the duchies handed Gottorp's mint to Hertug Adolph). Christian III's
  northern royal mint, runs 1545–1554+, mintmaster Jørgen Kock den
  Yngre. Galster: «Kongen anlagde derfor en ny kongelig mønt i
  Flensborg, hvorfra der i årene 1545-54 udgik rhinske gylden,
  dalere og mindre sølvmønt. Kun gennem daleren var der fællesskab
  i mønt med kongerigerne.»
  - Flensborg output: c3h15-c3h22 — Rhinsk Gylden, sølvgylden, søsling lybsk.
  - Flensborg's silver standard differs from København's — mf 8.982
    (sølvgylden 1545–1547) and mf 9.287 (søsling lybsk 1545–1554),
    reflecting Lübisch-zone parity rather than København's 1544
    baseline.
- **Provincial mints close.** The 1534–1536 Grevens fejde war-finance
  mints at Århus, Roskilde, Gottorp (under Christian III's pre-king
  Holstein duchy phase) wind down. Galster: «Al denne mønt nedsattes
  1537 og 1540 til det halve af det oprindelige.»

## 6. Pre-1541 antecedents

- **1518 first Danish Daler** (Christian II): «De første dalere blev
  udmøntet i 1518 af bjergsølv til erstatning for guldgylden» (Galster).
  Established the daler as a concept in Danish coinage but never
  codified by ordinance; rare specimens.
- **1534–1536 Grevens fejde Klippinger** (Christian III pre-king,
  emergency war finance): Klipping coins from Århus + Roskilde at
  irregular fineness. Demonetised 1537 / 1540.
- **1537 Joachimsdaler at København**: Christian III's first
  daler-format issue, anticipating the 1541 codification by 4 years.
  Single-issue precedent.
- **1540 Recess** — verbatim Wilcke quote (via Wilcke w1a.htm):
  «*oc schall een rhinsk Gylden eller en Jochimsdaler ikke dyrere
  wedtzles end for III Mark Danske…*» — i.e. one Rhenish Gulden or
  one Joachimstaler shall not be exchanged at more than III Mark
  Danske. This 1540 act sets the daler-vs-mark exchange ceiling that
  the 1541 ordinance implements as a formal coinage standard.

The 1541 Møntordning is therefore not an *invention* of the daler in
Denmark — the daler was already known (1518), already minted (1537)
and already tariffed (1540). The 1541 ordinance is the *formal codif­
ication* placing the daler at the head of a complete denomination
system, with a fixed mint location, a known mintmaster, and explicit
fineness/Marken-fin parameters.

## 7. Impact and legacy

- **Established the Danish silver-coinage system that ran 1541 → 1582.**
  Galster's three-phase map (Phase A 1541–1559 Christian III, Phase B
  1563–1570 Syvårskrigen Klipping, Phase C 1572+ post-Elfsborgs løsen
  9-Fuß alignment) sits entirely under the 1541 + 1544 framework
  until the 1582 successor ordinance.
- **1 Daler = 3 Mark Danske convention.** Lasted 1544 → 1582. The
  1582 Møntordning revises to 1 Daler = 4 Mark («efter mange års
  håbløs forvirring måtte man i møntordningen af 1582 vende tilbage
  til ‹mønten, som før gik› og dele daleren i 4 mark» — Galster); the
  1602 Møntordning further tariffs 1 Daler = 66 Skilling.
- **Parity with Schleswig-Holstein and Lübeck achieved through the
  daler.** Galster: «Kun gennem daleren var der fællesskab i mønt med
  kongerigerne» — only at the daler level was there common standard
  between the kingdom and the duchies (the Flensborg sølvgylden /
  søsling lybsk track addressed the Lübisch zone separately).
- **Coin-name-on-design** — durable. After 1541 this becomes standard
  Danish practice and persists through the entire period covered by
  our project (1559–1914).
- **Pre-imperial-alignment Danish standard.** The 1541 standard
  (~26.5 g fein per Daler, mf 8.827) is HEAVIER than the imperial
  9-Fuß (25.984 g fein, mf 9.000) that would later become the
  Reichsthaler benchmark. The 1544 supplement (~24.7 g, mf 9.481)
  goes ~5 % LIGHTER. Frederik II's 1572 «Elfsborgs løsen» Speciedaler
  (f2h17) is the first symbolic Danish issue aligned to the imperial
  framework. Christian IV's 1596+ Speciedalere (Hede c4h44) reach
  canonical 9-Fuß compliance (mf 9.000, 25.984 g per Daler at finhed
  0.888).

## 9. Where to find the verbatim ordinance text (future-research path)

If a session ever needs the actual Danish-language ordinance text (not
the secondary paraphrases used in this dossier):

1. **Kolderup-Rosenvinge, J. L. A.**: *Samling af gamle danske Love*,
   Vol. IV — *Danske Recesser og Ordinantser af Kongerne af den
   Oldenborgske Stamme* (København: Deichmann, 1824). Print
   collection of Oldenburg-dynasty royal ordinances; Vol. IV
   chronologically covers the Christian III / Frederik II era and
   would contain the 1541 ordinance verbatim. Google Books has an
   indexed entry (ID `LUVDAQAAMAAJ`) but snippet-only; full PDF not
   indexed on archive.org as of 2026-05-15. **Action**: source a
   paper or microfilm copy via inter-library loan, or scan a held
   library copy.
2. **Rigsarkivet, København — Danske Kancelli** archive series.
   Browsing index: [arkivalieronline.rigsarkivet.dk](https://arkivalieronline.rigsarkivet.dk/da/other/other-collection/149).
   The 1541 ordinance original would be in the Danske Kancelli
   series (Danish chancery; the parallel Tyske Kancelli series
   holds the duchies' German-language counterparts). Pre-1660
   Danske Kancelli is fully scanned and online; locating the 1541
   ordinance requires navigating to the appropriate sub-series and
   year. **Action**: open a session on Arkivalieronline, navigate
   Danske Kancelli → 1530s–1540s series → identify the September
   1541 entry.

Both paths produce the verbatim Old Danish text. Until a session
exercises either path, this dossier's §3–§4 paraphrased synthesis from
Galster / Wilcke / Kroman is the project's best available substitute.
